- Template: `historical_slow_build`

Numbers decide:
- seats in legislatures
- shares of budgets
- eligibility for rights

Getting counted can be power. Not getting counted can also be power.
3. **setup**  
Before modern headcounts, states tried cheaper proxies.

England's **Hearth Tax (1662)** counted fireplaces, not people-because households were easier to see than individuals, and easier to punish for underreporting.
4. **mechanism**  
Modern censuses build *legibility* with three tools:

1. **Categories** (race, occupation, residence)
2. **Enumerators** who knock on doors
3. **Registers** that link answers to addresses

Together they turn messy lives into governable lists.
5. **concrete_example**  
In **British India's 1871-72 census**, officials froze fluid communities into fixed "castes" and "tribes."

The point wasn't anthropology. It was administration: predict labor, collect revenue, and manage rebellion with standardized labels.
6. **consequence**  
Second-order effect: categories start steering reality.

In **Rwanda's 1933-34 census**, Belgian administrators formalized "Hutu/Tutsi/Twa" IDs.

A counting exercise became a social boundary-then a bureaucratic shortcut for discrimination and violence.
7. **closer**  
A census is a bargain: information for services and recognition.

When trust is low, people hide; when coercion is high, people comply.

So the real question isn't "How many?" It's "For what purpose?"

6. **second_order_effect**  
Because many bills matured together, traders started **netting** debts instead of paying gross sums.

At the **Lyon fairs**, this "clearing" meant fewer coins moved, yet far more trade could be settled.
7. **closer**  
Bills of exchange didn't just finance voyages; they standardized trust.

Second-order effect: **credibility became collateral**.

Sovereignty started to look like a balance sheet.
7. **closer**  
The lesson isn't "printing beats taxation."

Seigniorage works only when the sovereign can enforce a **trusted standard**.

Lose that trust, and money stops being revenue-and starts being a referendum.

### EV Isn't the Same as Smart

- Canonical topic: `Expected value thinking`
- Pillar: `decision_making_and_judgment`
- Template: `model_breakdown`

1. **hook**  
You can win a coin-flip bet and still have made a bad decision.

Expected value separates "got lucky" from "played it smart."
2. **definition**  
Expected value (EV) = Σ(probability × payoff).

It's the average result if you could run the same choice many times, with honest odds and outcomes.
3. **mechanism**  
Use EV like this:
- List outcomes + probabilities
- Multiply, then add
- Check **utility**: can you absorb losses?
- Ask "what info changes the odds?" (VOI)
4. **real-world example**  
Example: Rent is due.

A friend offers: 51% win **$200**, 49% lose **$200**.
EV = 0.51×200 - 0.49×200 = **+$4**.

If losing means eviction, it's a terrible bet.
5. **mistake_or_contrast**  
Common trap: treating EV as the answer.

EV works best when you can repeat the bet and never go bust.

Fat-tail risks (ruin, lawsuits, death) dominate.
6. **closer**  
Before saying "+EV, do it," ask:
1) What's my downside if I'm wrong?
2) Can I survive it?
3) What cheap info improves odds?

### The Hidden Price of "Yes"

- Canonical topic: `Opportunity cost`
- Pillar: `decision_making_and_judgment`
- Template: `model_breakdown`

1. **hook**  
That "quick call" is rarely quick.

The real bill isn't on the invoice-it's the better work you can't do while you're on it.

Swipe.
2. **definition**  
Opportunity cost = the value of the best option you give up when you choose something else.

Every decision "spends" money, time, attention, or momentum-whether you notice or not.
3. **mechanism**  
To use it:

1. Name the best alternative you'd do instead.  
2. Shadow-price your time: what does an hour buy in that alternative?  
3. Add cost of delay: what gets worse if you wait?
4. **real-world example**  
Example: you're a freelancer.

A client wants a 3-hour "urgent tweak" for $450.

Those 3 hours could finish a proposal with a 40% chance of landing a $3,000 project.
5. **mistake/contrast**  
Mistake: treating the tweak as "found money" because it's cash today.

Expected value of the proposal is $1,200, plus it starts sooner.

The cheap job is expensive.
6. **closer**  
Before you say yes, ask: "What is the best thing this replaces?"

If the trade isn't worth it at your shadow price (and delay cost), decline-or renegotiate the terms.

### Think Past the First Win

- Canonical topic: `Second-order effects`
- Pillar: `decision_making_and_judgment`
- Template: `model_breakdown`

1. **hook**  
You fixed the symptom.

Now the problem moved-and got more expensive.

That's the trap: first-order wins can create second-order losses. Swipe to spot them early.
2. **definition**  
Second-order effects are the consequences of consequences.

You don't just ask "What happens next?"

You ask "What happens because that happened?"
3. **mechanism**  
Mechanism: actions change incentives and constraints.

1) Direct result (1st order)
2) Behavior shifts in response (2nd order)
3) New equilibrium emerges (3rd order)
4. **real_world_example**  
Example: a store makes returns free to boost sales.

1st order: more people buy.

2nd order: customers over-order "to try," raising shipping waste and prices.
5. **mistake_or_contrast**  
Common mistake: stopping at "net positive."

If the incentive changes, expect gaming.

Contrast: test with guardrails-limits, friction, or eligibility-then watch how people adapt.
6. **closer**  
Before you decide, run this quick scan:

- Who will change behavior?
- What will they optimize for?
- What breaks if it works?

Good judgment is forecasting reactions.

### Premortem: Plan for the Crash

- Canonical topic: `Premortem planning`
- Pillar: `decision_making_and_judgment`
- Template: `model_breakdown`

1. **hook**  
Your plan looks solid-until reality runs it.

The trap: you only ask, "How do we win?"

Swipe to ask the scarier question first.
2. **definition**  
A premortem means you assume the project failed, then write the story of how it died.

You're not predicting doom-you're surfacing risks early.
3. **mechanism**  
Run it in 10 minutes:

1. **Fast-forward:** "It's 6 months later; we failed."
2. **Name 5 causes** (failure modes).
3. **Pick 2 fixes + 1 kill criterion** (stop-loss).
4. **real-world example**  
Example: you're launching a new pricing page.

Premortem says it "failed" because conversions dropped, support tickets spiked, and sales lost trust.

Fix: staged rollout. Kill: revert if trial-to-paid falls 15%.

Common misuse: turning premortems into anxiety theater.

- Vague fears ("competitors!")
- No owners
- No stop-loss

A good premortem ends with decisions, not more scenarios.
6. **closer**  
Before you commit money, time, or reputation, do one premortem.

Invite a friendly skeptic to "red-team" it.

Then write your fixes and your exit condition.

Split decisions into two buckets:

3. **mechanism**  
How it works:

1) Pick a reference class.
2) Use its base rates (median, spread).

### Stop Overreacting to Evidence

- Canonical topic: `Bayesian updating`
- Pillar: `decision_making_and_judgment`
- Template: `model_breakdown`

1. **hook**  
A test says **"positive"** and your brain jumps to **99% certain**.

That leap is how smart people panic. Swipe to fix it with Bayesian updating.
2. **definition**  
**Bayesian updating** means:

Start with a **prior belief** (the base rate), then adjust it using new evidence-**weighted by reliability**-to get a new probability.
3. **mechanism**  
Use this 3-step update:

1) **Prior:** what's typical here?
2) **Likelihood:** how often does this evidence show up if true vs false?
3) **Posterior:** prior × evidence strength.
4. **real-world example**  
Example:

A disease hits **1 in 1,000** people. A test is **99% accurate**. You test positive.

Your chance isn't ~99%-it's closer to **~9%**.
5. **mistake/contrast**  
Common mistake: treating evidence as a **verdict**.

If your prior is shaky, do **prior sensitivity**:
- try **1/500**
- try **1/2,000**

Weak evidence shouldn't swing you hard.
6. **closer**  
Bayesian updating is **disciplined doubt**.

Ask:
- "What was likely **before** this?"
- "How surprising is this evidence?"

Update in small steps-your decisions stop whiplashing.

Calibration means your confidence matches your hit-rate.

3. **mechanism**  
Do it with *prediction ranges*:

1. State a 90% interval (low-high).
2. Record outcomes.

- You give a single date ("May 17").
- You backfill reasons when late.
- You never change the next estimate.

6. **closer**  
Try this for a week:

1. Make 3 forecasts/day with 90% ranges.
2. Track hits in a note.
3. If you're under 90%, widen.

Trust follows accuracy *and* honesty.

- 3 vetoes

### When Averages Lie

- Canonical topic: `Fat tails and power laws`
- Pillar: `risk_uncertainty_and_resilience`
- Template: `model_breakdown`

1. **hook**  
If you invest in 20 startups, 19 can fail and you still beat the market.

That's not luck-it's the math of fat tails. Swipe.
2. **definition**  
Fat tails: extreme outcomes happen far more often than a normal bell curve predicts.

Power law: impact scales like **1/x^k**-a few events drive most results.
3. **mechanism**  
In power-law worlds, totals are **tail-dominated**.

The biggest outcome isn't an outlier; it's the main contributor.

Variance can be infinite, so "average" stops being informative.
4. **real-world example**  
VC funds expect this.

One unicorn can return the entire fund; the rest are write-offs.

That's why they seek huge upside, not "pretty good" companies.
5. **mistake/contrast**  
Mistake: modeling fat-tail domains with normal assumptions-using **mean ± standard deviation**.

You'll underprice downside (blow-ups) and undervalue upside (home runs), mis-sizing bets both ways.
6. **closer**  
Ask: **is this tail-dominated?**

If yes: cap ruin (limits, insurance, no leverage) and keep many small shots at massive upside.

Optimize for survival + optionality.

Margin of safety = the buffer between what you need and what you have.

Why it works:

- Reality has variance.
- Models miss edge cases.

**Survivorship bias**: judging a strategy's risk using only the visible winners.

The failures are missing from the sample, so the odds look better than they are.
3. **mechanism**  
When outcomes are filtered, your brain confuses *selection* with *skill*.

### The Lemons Trap

- Pillar: `incentives_power_and_coordination`

1. **hook**  
That "too-good" used car listing?

2. **definition**  
**Adverse selection** happens when one side knows more than the other *before* the deal.

3. **mechanism**  
Mechanism (lemons spiral):

1) Buyers fear hidden defects → offer "average" prices.

- inspections, certifications, warranties
- escrow or return windows
- reputation systems

1. **hook**  
You want to be the "reasonable" player. So does the other side.

Yet you both end up worse off-because the safest move is the one that ruins trust.
2. **definition**  
Prisoner's dilemma dynamics: when each person's best response is to defect, even though mutual cooperation would pay both more.

Individual incentives overpower the shared outcome.
3. **mechanism**  
**Mechanism**
- If you cooperate and they defect, you eat the loss.
- If you defect and they cooperate, you win.

So "defect" becomes the dominant strategy → mutual defection.
4. **real-world example**  
Example: two gas stations on one corner.

Both prefer steady prices. But if one cuts 5¢, it steals traffic. The other matches. Margins vanish, drivers barely notice.
5. **mistake/contrast**  
Contrast: one-shot vs repeated.

In a repeated game, retaliation is possible. Tit-for-tat (cooperate first, then mirror) makes defection costly tomorrow-often stabilizing cooperation today.
6. **closer**  
Use it deliberately:
- Extend the horizon (contracts, renewals).
- Track reputation publicly.
- Make cheating quickly detectable.

Cooperation isn't kindness; it's incentives engineered for repeated play.

2. **definition**  
In a coordination game, you win by matching others' choices.

3. **mechanism**  
Mechanism: when mismatch is costly, groups pay a coordination tax.

Mistake: arguing "best specs" while ignoring predictability.

6. **closer**  
Closer: want coordination?

Don't ask for opinions-set a default, name the standard, and make it public.

The fastest way to change behavior is to change expectations.

Your BATNA is the best alternative you'll take if this deal fails.

Bargaining power comes from options: the other side must pay to keep you from choosing your BATNA.

Before any negotiation, write two numbers:

1. your BATNA in plain language
2. your reservation value in dollars/time

### Stop Optimizing the Wrong Step

- Canonical topic: `Bottlenecks and constraints`
- Pillar: `systems_operations_and_constraints`
- Template: `model_breakdown`

1. **hook**  
You hired two more people... and customers still wait.

Because the slowest step sets the pace. Until you find it, "improvements" just create bigger piles.
2. **definition**  
A **constraint** is the single part of a system that most limits output.

All other steps either feed it, starve it, or build inventory in front of it.
3. **mechanism**  
**TOC in 3 moves (Drum-Buffer-Rope):**
1) Set the **drum**: schedule to the constraint's rate.
2) Protect it with a **buffer**.
3) Use a **rope**: release work only when buffer needs it.
4. **real-world example**  
Example: a warehouse ships 500 orders/day.

Picking can do 800. Packing can do 500.
Make **packing** the drum, keep a 1-hour tote buffer, and stop releasing new picks when it's full.
5. **mistake/contrast**  
Common trap: "Speed up picking" with more scanners.

Result: aisles clogged, packing buried, errors rise.
If packing improves, re-check-today's constraint may hop to **labeling** or **carrier pickup**.
6. **closer**  
Quick test:
- Where does work **queue** every day?
- Which step is never idle?

Improve that first. Everything else is support, not the mission.

### Why More Work Slows You Down

- Canonical topic: `Little’s law`
- Pillar: `systems_operations_and_constraints`
- Template: `model_breakdown`

1. **hook**  
You hire more agents, add more tickets, and somehow customers wait longer.

The trap: thinking "more in the pipeline" means "faster delivery."
2. **definition**  
Little's law connects three averages:

**Lead time = WIP / Throughput**

WIP = items in progress. Throughput = items finished per time. Lead time = average time to finish.
3. **mechanism**  
If throughput stays flat, every extra in-progress item becomes waiting time.

Queues form at the constraint, not where you feel busy. Reduce WIP, and the same flow clears faster.
4. **real-world example**  
Support inbox example:

- 120 open tickets (WIP)
- 30 tickets closed/day (throughput)

Lead time ≈ **120/30 = 4 days**.
Cut WIP to 60 ⇒ ~2 days.
5. **mistake/misuse/contrast**  
Where Little's law breaks (or you misapply it):

- You count "started" work, not "truly in progress."
- Throughput swings (new hires, outages, seasonality).
- Items aren't comparable (1-minute vs 1-week tickets).
6. **closer**  
Operational move:

1. Measure average throughput for the constraint.
2. Set a visible WIP cap.
3. Don't start new work until something finishes.

Lead time drops without "working harder."

Queueing + variability: when arrivals and work times fluctuate, a system forms a line.

Mechanism:
- Variability creates surges.

### Why Systems Swing Out of Control

- Canonical topic: `Feedback loops`
- Pillar: `systems_operations_and_constraints`
- Template: `model_in_action`

1. **hook**  
Your team "fixes" a backlog fast... then everything collapses.

That whiplash isn't bad luck. It's a feedback loop you accidentally amplified. Swipe.
2. **definition**  
A **feedback loop** is when today's actions change tomorrow's conditions, which then change what you do next.

Two types: **reinforcing** (more→more) and **balancing** (pushback→stability).
3. **mechanism**  
Loops get dangerous with **delays**.

You act on old information, so you overcorrect.

Result: oscillations-up, down, up-until the system hits a hard constraint (cash, capacity, morale).

Example: a retailer sees shelves empty, so they double next week's order.

The common mistake: treating symptoms as causes.

Empty shelves ≠ "we need more stock"; it might be "we need faster replenishment."

Discounts are a **fix that backfires**-they distort the signal.
6. **closer**  
When you see swings, map the loop:

1) What output are we reacting to?
2) What delay hides reality?
3) What constraint limits response?

Then choose a balancing move, not a bigger push.

### Backlogs Are Stocks, Not Mysteries

- Canonical topic: `Stock-and-flow thinking`
- Pillar: `systems_operations_and_constraints`

1. **hook**  
Your support backlog can triple even while everyone works harder.

2. **definition**  
Stock-and-flow thinking separates:
- **Stock** = what's accumulated (open tickets)
- **Flow** = rates changing it (new tickets/day, tickets/day solved).
3. **mechanism**  
Backlog tomorrow = backlog today + inflow - outflow.

If inflow is 120/day and your bottleneck team can only clear 100/day, the stock grows 20/day-guaranteed.

You add a chatbot and cut average handle time. Nice.

Common mistake: attacking the **stock** with a one-time cleanup sprint.

If rates stay the same, the pile returns. Fix the constraint that limits outflow, or reduce inflow.
6. **closer**  
When something "keeps piling up," ask three questions:
1) What's the stock?
2) What are inflow/outflow rates?
3) Where's the bottleneck?

Change rates, not wishes.

### When Metrics Lie

- Canonical topic: `Correlation vs causation`
- Pillar: `causality_measurement_and_learning`
- Template: `model_breakdown`

1. **hook**  
Your metric jumped after a change.

The dashboard screams **"win."**

But the same jump might have happened if you did nothing.
2. **definition**  
**Correlation** means two things move together.

**Causation** means changing A would change B.

Predictive models love correlation; decision-making needs causation.
3. **mechanism**  
Why correlation appears:

- **A → B** (real effect)
- **B → A** (reverse causality)
- **C → A & B** (confounder)
- pure chance (noise)
4. **real-world example**  
Example: Users who enable push notifications retain **2×** longer.

Tempting conclusion: notifications **cause** retention.

Reality: the most engaged users opt in, so retention **causes** opt-in.
5. **mistake_or_contrast**  
Misuse: shipping more notifications because the model **predicts** retention.

Fix: test causality-randomize opt-in prompts, compare holdouts, and check timing (does retention rise **after** enabling?).
6. **closer**  
Before you celebrate a correlation, ask: **"What's the counterfactual?"**

If you can't answer, you don't have a lever yet-just a hint about where to experiment.

### The Hidden Third Variable

- Canonical topic: `Confounding and omitted variables`
- Pillar: `causality_measurement_and_learning`
- Template: `model_breakdown`

1. **hook**  
Your new pricing changed nothing-yet revenue jumped.

Before you celebrate, ask: what else changed at the same time that could be driving both?
2. **definition**  
A confounder is a hidden factor that influences *both* your supposed cause and your outcome.

Omit it, and correlation starts wearing causality's costume.
3. **mechanism**  
Mechanism:
1) **X** and **Y** move together.
2) **Z** pushes **X** and **Y**.
3) You credit **X → Y**, but **Z** is the engine.
4. **real-world example**  
Ice cream sales correlate with drownings.

Ice cream doesn't drown people-summer heat increases swimming *and* ice cream buying.

Heat is the confounder.
5. **mistake/contrast**  
Common mistake: "We compared customers who chose the new plan vs. old."

That choice filters for motivation, budget, and need.

That's selection bias-an omitted-variable factory.
6. **closer**  
Fix it by hunting **Z**:
- Randomize / A/B test when possible
- Measure likely confounders
- Compare like-with-like (controls/matching)

If you can't, say "association," not "impact."

### Stop Controlling the Wrong Variable

- Canonical topic: `Causal diagrams (DAGs)`
- Pillar: `causality_measurement_and_learning`
- Template: `model_breakdown`

1. **hook**  
Your A/B test says the new checkout increases revenue.

Then you "control for" returning customers-and the effect vanishes.

You didn't find truth. You opened a trapdoor.
2. **definition**  
A causal diagram (DAG) is a map of cause → effect arrows with no cycles.

It tells you which variables create bias, and which variables you're allowed to adjust for.
3. **mechanism**  
Estimate **X → Y** by blocking every **backdoor path** between them.

- Adjust for common causes (confounders).
- Never adjust for a **collider**-a variable caused by both X and Y.
4. **real-world example**  
Example: Does paid search increase sales?

- **X:** Paid search spend
- **Y:** Sales
- **Z:** Seasonality (affects both)

Adjust for **seasonality** to close the backdoor.
5. **mistake/contrast**  
Common mistake: adjusting for **clicks**.

Spend → Clicks → Sales is the pathway you're trying to measure.

Control for clicks and you estimate only the leftover effect, not total impact.
6. **closer**  
Before running a regression, draw the DAG on paper.

Ask:
1) What causes **X** and **Y**?
2) What sits **on the path**?
3) What's a **collider**?

### RCTs: Stop Guessing Causes

- Canonical topic: `Randomized controlled trials`
- Pillar: `causality_measurement_and_learning`
- Template: `model_breakdown`

1. **hook**  
Your new feature "won" by 12%.

Then you discover sales called the big accounts first.

Congrats-you didn't run an experiment. You ran a story.
2. **definition**  
A **randomized controlled trial (RCT)** assigns people to **Treatment** or **Control** by chance, then compares outcomes.

Randomization makes groups comparable, so differences are plausibly **caused** by the treatment.
3. **mechanism**  
Mechanism:
- Randomize assignment *before* exposure.
- Run **randomization checks** (balance on age, baseline spend).
- Analyze **intent-to-treat**: keep everyone in their assigned group, even if they don't comply.
4. **real-world example**  
Example: A clinic tests **SMS appointment reminders**.
- 5,000 patients randomly get SMS or usual calls.
- No baseline differences.
- Missed appointments drop 3 points in SMS group → causal effect.
5. **mistake/contrast**  
Common misuse:
- Reporting **per-protocol** ("only those who read the SMS") inflates impact; compliers differ.
- Some questions can't be randomized ethically (you can't assign smoking).
6. **closer**  
RCTs don't guarantee good decisions-they guarantee **cleaner learning**.

Ask:
1) Was assignment truly random?
2) Are you analyzing intent-to-treat?
3) If you can't randomize, what's your next-best design?

### The Sophomore Slump Trap

- Canonical topic: `Regression to the mean`
- Pillar: `causality_measurement_and_learning`
- Template: `model_breakdown`

1. **hook**  
Your top salesperson has a record month.

You promote their "secret script." Next month numbers fall.

The script didn't break-your interpretation did. Swipe.
2. **definition**  
Regression to the mean: when a result is unusually high or low, the next measurement tends to move closer to the long-run average-even if nothing changes.
3. **mechanism**  
Performance = skill + noise.

Extreme outcomes usually include extra noise.

When the noise fades, you see more of the true baseline, so results "revert."
4. **real-world example**  
Example: a rookie shoots 45% from three for two months, gets hyped as an elite sniper.

Next season they're at 35%. Same player; luck cooled.
5. **mistake / contrast**  
The trap: you credit a new coach, diet, or punishment for the drop-or the rise.

Contrast: causal change shifts the *average* over many games, not one streak.
6. **closer**  
Before "learning," ask:

- Was this extreme?
- What's the base rate?

Track a moving average, not a highlight. Intervene only when trends persist.

### Stop Managing the Noise

- Canonical topic: `Measurement error and noise`
- Pillar: `causality_measurement_and_learning`
- Template: `model_breakdown`

1. **hook**  
Your dashboard updates every minute.

So you "fix" the business every minute.

That isn't agility. It's noise-driven management.
2. **definition**  
Measurement error (noise) is the gap between what you observe and what's true.

**Observed = True signal + Noise.**

When noise is big, small "moves" are illusions.
3. **mechanism**  
Noise isn't just randomness. It has sources:

- sampling (who showed up)
- instrument error (tracking, sensors)
- process drift (day-of-week effects)

Reduce it by repeating, averaging, or redesigning measurement.
4. **real-world example**  
You ship a new checkout button. Next day, conversion drops from 3.0% to 2.8%.

But day-to-day conversion normally swings ±0.4%.

The "drop" is inside noise.
5. **mistake/contrast**  
Common trap: treating one measurement as a verdict.

- reacting to a single day
- declaring winners from tiny A/B lifts

Better: ask **repeatability** (same setup) and **reproducibility** (different days/segments).
6. **closer**  
Before you change course, run this:

1) What's the typical noise band?
2) Is today's shift outside it?
3) How many repeats would make you confident?

Decisions need signal, not "fresh" data.

### Measure Moves, Not Scores

- Canonical topic: `Leading vs lagging indicators`
- Pillar: `causality_measurement_and_learning`
- Template: `model_breakdown`

1. **hook**  
You hit the quarterly number-and still built a weaker business.

Because you measured the score, not the moves that create it. Swipe to stop flying blind.
2. **definition**  
Lagging indicators tell you what happened (revenue, weight, NPS).

Leading indicators are controllable actions that predict the lag later (calls made, workouts, response time).
3. **mechanism**  
Causal chain:

1. Inputs you control → 2. Behaviors → 3. Outcomes.

Lead metrics live earlier in the chain, so they change faster-and give you time to correct. That link decays when conditions change.
4. **real-world example**  
Example: reducing churn.

Lagging: churn rate this month.
Leading: % tickets answered in <2 hours, and follow-ups completed.
Track leading weekly; watch churn monthly.
5. **mistake / contrast**  
Common trap: treating a lead metric as a trophy.

If reps rush replies to hit "2 hours," customers get wrong fixes.
A "leading" metric without causality becomes noise. And yesterday's predictor can stop predicting.
6. **closer**  
Use both:

- Pick 1 lagging "scoreboard" metric.
- Choose 2-3 leading actions you can do daily.
- Re-test the link quarterly; retire stale indicators.

Measure to learn, not to win.

### When Metrics Lie

- Canonical topic: `Proxy metrics and metric validity`
- Pillar: `causality_measurement_and_learning`
- Template: `model_breakdown`

1. **hook**  
Your dashboard is up.

Customers are leaving.

That's not bad luck-it's a proxy metric drifting away from the real outcome. Swipe to spot it early.
2. **definition**  
A **proxy metric** is a measurable stand-in for what you truly care about.

**Metric validity (construct validity)** asks: does moving this number *actually mean* the outcome improved?
3. **mechanism**  
Proxies fail when the link to the outcome changes:

- you optimize the proxy, not the cause
- people game it
- context shifts, so correlation decays
4. **real-world example**  
Call centers chase **Average Handle Time**.

Agents rush calls, transfers rise, customers call back.

AHT looks great, but repeat contacts and churn climb.
5. **mistake/misuse/contrast**  
Mistake: treating one proxy as truth.

Fix: **triangulate**.
- pair AHT with first-contact resolution
- watch complaint rate
- sample real call quality
6. **closer**  
Before you ship a metric target, ask:

*How could this number rise while the customer gets worse?*

If you can answer, add guardrails-or pick a better proxy.

### The A/B Test Trap

- Canonical topic: `A/B testing pitfalls`
- Pillar: `causality_measurement_and_learning`
- Template: `model_breakdown`

1. **hook**  
Day 2: Variant B is up 18%.

You ship it. Week 2: revenue drops.

What happened? Your A/B test didn't lie-you asked it the wrong way.
2. **definition**  
A/B testing pitfalls are ways experiments create false "winners":

- peeking mid-run
- multiple comparisons
- mistaking novelty or seasonality for real lift
3. **mechanism**  
Each time you peek, you give luck another chance to look "significant".

Add more variants or metrics, and the false-positive rate compounds-until something wins by accident.
4. **real-world example**  
Example: You test a "Free Shipping Today" banner.

It jumps on Friday night. You stop early and roll out.

Next week the lift vanishes: payday traffic + novelty, not impact.
5. **mistake_or_contrast**  
Misuse: "We'll run it until it's green."

Better:

- pre-set sample size + duration
- choose one primary metric
- correct for multiple comparisons
- cover full weekly cycles
6. **closer**  
Treat A/B tests like thermometers: they work only when used consistently.

If you can't explain stopping rules, metrics, and number of looks, you're not learning-you're gambling.

### Prove It Outside the Data

- Canonical topic: `Out-of-sample validation`
- Pillar: `causality_measurement_and_learning`
- Template: `model_breakdown`

1. **hook**  
Your dashboard says the campaign "worked." Next week you rerun it-and the lift vanishes.

That's not bad luck. That's a model that never left its training data.
2. **definition**  
Out-of-sample validation = test your claim on data it didn't see while you built it.

If it still holds, it's more likely signal, not storytelling.
3. **mechanism**  
Mechanism:
1. Split: train vs. test (or future holdout).
2. Lock choices: features, thresholds, metrics.
3. Score once on test.

Pre-register the plan to reduce "just one more tweak."
4. **real-world example**  
Example: you predict which users will buy after an email.

Train on January sends.
Test on February sends.

If accuracy drops sharply, you learned the January pattern-not buying behavior.
5. **mistake_misuse_contrast**  
Common failure: leakage.

You include "opened email" or "clicked" as predictors-events that happen *after* sending.

In-sample it looks brilliant. Out-of-sample it collapses in production.
6. **closer**  
Closer: treat your test set like a sealed exam.
- Don't tune on it
- Don't peek repeatedly
- Refresh with new time periods

A result that survives holdouts is worth betting on.
